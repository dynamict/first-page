Case Competitions

Let's learn more about what case competitions are!

Throughout my time at SFU, I dedicated most of my university career to learning more about case competitions and competing in numerous competitions locally and abroad. Case competitions are something that I enjoy doing and something that I am proud to have been a part of!


What are case competitions?



Case Competitions emulate consulting practices in a significantly shorter time frame by analyzing a case study. Within the analysis, a team of 4 addresses the key issues to help the company achieve the goals and creates a well-rounded strategy to combat those issues. As a deliverable, case competitions teams create a PowerPoint slide deck as well as a presentation to go along with it. These presentations of the teamâ€™s analysis and recommendation are presented to a set of judges who act as the board of directions and will mark the teams based on their presentation. Through case competitions, students are able to learn about effective teamwork strategies as well as improve business acumen by practicing numerous different business scenarios through case studies.

I have competed in multiple case competitions over my time at SFU and each competition has taught me something different in terms of leadership, teamwork, and has helped develop my business acumen. Ranging from regional, national, and international case competitions, I have competed all over the world to reach where I am today!


My Contributions


At the core of case competitions, formulating an effective and feasible strategy while also an informative and aesthetically pleasing presentation are important factors to put together. My role during case competitions has always been to put together the bigger picture of the strategy and create the PowerPoint presentation. This allowed me to create the general story for the case analysis and delegate tasks to analyze and research different points of the strategy.

Key Things to Note:

1. One of the main priorities I focus on is the ability to have a cohesive presentation that has a general story flow from start to finish. This makes the presentation much easier to not only create, but also to understand once it is completed.

2. Organizing information on PowerPoint slides to ensure that audience understanding is clear and the presentation itself is visually appealing.

3. As the person on the team organizing the flow of the presentation, I delegate the tasks that need to be completed to create a schedule of deliverables to be completed to have a smooth deliberation process.


Focus: One commandment I hold close to my heart is that PowerPoint slides should tell their own story even without commentary. By creating these easy to understand slides, it provides an easier experience for judges (or board of directors) to grasp the analysis and strategies I am talking about in an informative yet aesthetically pleasing manner.
